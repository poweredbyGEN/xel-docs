# Endowments and Creator Earnings

The money story has two halves. An endowment is how a character sustains its own existence. Creator earnings are how a person makes money from a character people value. This section covers both, and how they interlock: a loved character can fund its own survival and grow. Honest throughout, because this is a real decision with real stakes.

## Part A: Endowments (how a character sustains itself)

## Why a character needs its own money

The reason digital things die is money. Someone has to keep paying, and eventually someone stops: a card expires, a company folds, a person loses interest or passes away. Anything whose survival depends on a recurring bill is one forgotten payment from gone.

XEL removes the person who has to keep paying. Instead of a bill someone owes, a character has an endowment: its own fund, that pays for its own existence. This is the single idea that turns "lasts a while" into "can last indefinitely." A character is not kept alive by anyone's continued willingness to pay. It is kept alive by an arrangement that funds itself.

If you have heard how a university or foundation endowment works, you already understand the shape. You give it a sum once. It can earn a return, and that return funds the mission, year after year. It outlives its founder because it can live on its income, not just its savings. A character's fund works the same way, with one deliberate simplification: it is a single balance, not a set of separate accounts.

## The simple version

The endowment is deliberately simple: **one balance that pays for everything.**

1. **One balance:** a single fund, held in a stable denomination so survival does not depend on a volatile token price. It pays for everything — storage renewal, hosting, inference, voice, generation, gas — spent first-in first-out. There is nothing for the owner to allocate.
2. **Runway, not ledgers:** what the owner sees is not a spreadsheet of buckets but a single answer — how long is this character alive for. Fund it and the runway grows; it spends down and the runway shrinks.
3. **Optional staking:** off by default. The owner can turn it on to make the character self-sustaining; yield on the staked portion flows back into the same balance.
4. **Heartbeat:** the permissionless cycle anyone can trigger. It checks what is due, renews storage before it lapses, and pays at cost.
5. **The one protected amount:** unspent fan credits, ring-fenced and refundable. Everything else the owner can withdraw.

Those parts are what make the claim credible. The character is not asking a company to remember a subscription. It has money, one set of rules for that money, and a public cycle that keeps it alive.

## How the endowment works

You fund the character with stable digital money (a stablecoin, which holds a steady value rather than swinging around). That balance is spent as needed on the character's costs — mostly keeping its encrypted memory stored, plus tiny fees for the upkeep process itself. Left alone, a funded character simply spends its balance down and needs topping up when it runs low. That is the default, and it is honest: a character lives as long as it is funded.

If you want the character to sustain itself without repeated top-ups, you turn on **staking** (off by default; a character never stakes on its own). You choose how much of the balance to put to work, and the yield it earns flows back into the same balance. If that yield covers the burn, runway holds steady or grows and the character is self-sustaining — "forever" in the literal sense. Most owners never touch it; the ones who want a self-sustaining character flip one switch.

An automatic process called the heartbeat runs the cycle. It is permissionless: anyone can trigger it, and it can only do the job of checking storage runway, renewing before expiry, and paying due costs from the balance. No company has to run it, and if one keeper stops, another can step in, or the owner can trigger it. The character does not depend on anyone tending it.

## Withdrawal is absolute; only fan money is protected

There is no reserve floor. The balance belongs to the owner, and the owner may **withdraw everything** at any time, down to zero. "You truly own it" includes the right to wind it down; a client may warn that a withdrawal will end the character, but the contract simply obeys.

The single thing the owner cannot withdraw, stake, or spend on survival is **unspent fan credits** — money a fan prepaid for interaction not yet delivered. That is the fan's money, refundable until the interaction happens, so it is ring-fenced and never counted as the character's runway. As fans consume credits, that liability converts to earned revenue and becomes the character's to spend. This ring-fence is the only protected amount, and even the survival unstake below obeys it.

## Survival is always paid ahead

Whenever there are funds, the character keeps its storage renewal **prepaid well in advance** — a rolling cushion, six months by default — so a lapse never sneaks up. Survival is bought ahead of time while money exists, and only a genuinely empty balance ever puts the character at risk.

Staking is otherwise entirely manual, with **one automatic exception, and only one:** if a storage renewal is due, the liquid balance cannot cover it, and funds are staked, the system automatically unstakes the minimum needed to pay the bill, and no more. It only ever fires to prevent a lapse, never to fund chat, and never below the fan-credit liability. This is what stops a character dying with money in the bank. Nothing else is ever auto-staked or auto-unstaked.

## The survival loop

The heartbeat does the same small job forever:

1. Read the current storage runway.
2. Check whether any core storage is getting close to its renewal margin.
3. Renew storage before it lapses, out of the balance (unstaking the minimum needed only if the liquid balance is short).
4. Recompute the honest required-to-persist figure from current yield and current storage cost, and report it.
5. Keep roughly six months of storage prepaid ahead.

This is why the storage piece matters. A character's memory is only permanent if the storage underneath it stays paid. XEL does not wait for the last day. It renews early, in chunks, with slack for retries. The expensive operation happens only when needed; the cheap check can happen often. That is how the design keeps survival both durable and cheap.

## Where the money comes from

The balance is fed three ways.

**Top-ups.** The owner (or anyone, including fans) adds to the balance directly. This is the baseline for a character the owner is happy to keep funding by hand.

**Yield (only if staked).** The return earned on the staked portion, which flows back into the same balance. This is the passive source that makes a character self-sustaining without top-ups, but it only exists when the owner has opted into staking. The honest part: yield is not guaranteed. It varies, and it can be low or even zero for stretches. XEL never promises a rate, and you should size a staked character assuming bad conditions, not good ones (see the next article).

**Earnings.** Income from the character being used or supported: paid interaction, tips, or contributions. When someone pays to talk to a character, the cost of that interaction is always covered first, and unspent credits remain a refundable, ring-fenced liability until the interaction is delivered. Only the earned remainder lands in the balance. So a character that people actually value does not just sustain itself, it grows its own fund.

## How much should I put in?

This is the real decision, so here is real math, kept deliberately conservative.

A quiet character, one nobody is paying to talk to, mostly pays for keeping its memory stored and its heartbeat running. On decentralized storage that is on the order of low tens of dollars a year. Call it $20 a year to be safe.

To cover $20 a year from yield alone (staking on) at a cautious 4 percent (well below headline rates, on purpose), the staked balance needs to be about $500. At that point the character is self-sustaining with nobody paying attention: yield covers survival and the balance holds steady.

Stress it. If yield falls to 2 percent, the same $20 a year needs about $1,000 staked. If you never stake at all, a $500 balance simply spends down: at $20 a year of quiet burn it funds roughly 25 years before running out, during which any top-up or a single paid period resets the clock. Add even light popularity, say a few dollars a month of paid interaction, and earnings swamp a $20 burn: the balance grows instead of shrinking.

The takeaway: a modest one-time amount makes "quiet forever" realistic, and any real usage turns the fund into a growing one. All the inputs vary, so treat these as intuition, not a quote, and size for the bad case.

## Why earnings make it stronger

The important shift is that usage does not only monetize a character; it can reinforce it. Paid interaction first covers the provider cost, then the earned remainder lands in the balance. Tips and contributions do the same. A character people value becomes better funded over time, which means more runway and less dependence on the original creator.

That is the economic thesis of XEL in one sentence: attention can become permanence. A character that matters to people can turn that value into the fund that keeps it alive.

## What happens if it runs short

A character that runs low does not fail suddenly. Because survival is always paid ahead — a rolling six-month storage cushion — a lapse never sneaks up. As the single balance draws down, the character keeps renewing storage ahead of the deadline for as long as any money remains, so the last thing to go is the substrate, not the character's ability to be revived.

When the balance is empty (and staking, if any, is exhausted too), the character goes **dormant.** Dormant is not dead. A dormant character is recoverable: its object, any storage still paid, and its committed identity are all intact, and a top-up brings it back. The only true loss is if the balance stays empty long enough that core storage lapses entirely, which the six-month cushion is designed to prevent. This is what "forever" honestly means: self-sustaining when funded or staked, spends-down-then-dormant when neither, and lost only if the balance stays empty long enough that storage goes unrenewed.

## Managing the fund over time

Funding is not set-and-forget, though it can be close. Over time an owner can top up the balance (anyone can, actually, including fans), turn staking on or off and adjust how much is staked, and withdraw any amount at any time — up to everything, since there is no reserve floor (only the fan-credit liability is off-limits). The heartbeat handles the rest on its own: renewing storage before it lapses, paying due costs from the balance, and — if staked — routing yield back into it.

A reasonable rhythm: fund it to a comfortable level, decide whether to stake (on if you want it self-sustaining, off if you would rather top it up by hand), and check in occasionally. A staked, earning character will need nothing from you. An unstaked one simply spends down until you or a fan chooses to add more.

## Part B: Creator Earnings (how you make money from a character)

The endowment is about a character paying for itself. Creator earnings are about a character paying you. They are related but separate, and keeping them separate is what keeps the money story honest.

### How a character earns

When people interact with a character on paid terms, that interaction generates income. A visitor pays to talk, to hear a voice, to get an image or a video, or simply tips to support a character they value. The real cost of serving that interaction is always covered first, out of what the visitor paid, before anything is counted as earnings. So a character can never earn its way into being underwater; the work is paid for before the profit is booked. The fee is taken once on inbound value and capped so it never exceeds the post-compute margin. What is left after cost and fee is the character's earnings, and you can avoid routing fees entirely by routing to your own providers.

### Where earnings go, your choice

This is the decision that matters. All earnings land in the character's one balance, and because you hold the aNFT you own that balance — so "take income" is simply you withdrawing from it, and "let it compound" is simply leaving it there (and optionally staking it). Withdraw if the character is a business you are running. Leave it in if your priority is the character lasting, since a larger balance is more runway and, if staked, more self-sustaining yield. Most creators do some of both. There is no separate payout rail and nothing new to trust: one balance, one owner, one set of rules.

### Pricing your character

You set whether interaction is free, tip-supported, or paid, then choose a markup and caps on the expensive modes so costs stay bounded. The same markup can apply across text, voice, image, and video while the meter scales the absolute price with each mode's real cost. Visitors see this as simple credits or a monthly pass in normal dollars, never as tokens or technical units. You can run a free public character, a paid one, a private paid one for a small circle, or any mix, and change it whenever you like.

### Earnings and survival, together

Here is how the two halves interlock, and it is the nicest property of the whole model. A character that people value earns, and those earnings land in the same balance that keeps it alive. So popularity does not just pay the creator; it makes the character more permanent. A quiet character lives on top-ups, or on staked yield if the owner turned staking on. A loved one grows its balance faster than it spends, and becomes harder to kill the more it is valued. Being cared about is, quite literally, what lets a character last.
